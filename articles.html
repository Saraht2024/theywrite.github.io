To the Ones Who Are On Their Own (But Not Alone)

Personal Essay • 5 min read • March 15, 2024

Hello to you. This is for the nights when it's too quiet. When the world seems made for two and you find yourself there, only you. When every couple walking hand in hand makes your chest ache a little.

This is for the Friday evenings when your phone goes silent and you wonder if this is what loneliness feels like, or if it's just the space between what was and what might be.

"You have not been overlooked. You have not wasted your time. You are not too much. And you are most definitely not 'not enough'."

Being single is not a waiting room. It is not the in-between nor the pause that precedes the actual thing happening. It is the thing itself. It's real. It counts. It's yours.

You are not half of anything. You are not incomplete. You are not a rough draft waiting for someone else to edit you into something better. You are the full story, written in your own handwriting, with plot twists that surprise even you.

The Art of Being Alone

There's an art to being alone that no one teaches you. It's not the same as loneliness, though sometimes they blur together like watercolors in the rain. Being alone is active. It's choosing yourself, again and again, even when the world suggests that two is better than one.

You learn things about yourself that you never knew when someone else was in the room. You discover that you actually like your own company. That your thoughts are interesting. That your dreams don't need validation to be valid.

You learn that you can take yourself to dinner, to movies, to new cities. You can be your own plus-one to life.

Some nights, you'll feel the weight of it. The silence will feel too loud, the bed too big, the apartment too still. Those nights are real too. They don't cancel out the good ones. They're just part of the story you're writing.

You Are Not Waiting

This is not a consolation prize. This is not the universe's way of preparing you for something better. This is your life, happening right now, in real time. Full and valid and complete.

Every day you choose to show up for yourself is a day that counts. Every small joy you find on your own is proof that happiness doesn't require a witness to be real. Every challenge you face and overcome alone builds a strength that no one can take away from you.

You are not practicing for the main event. This is the main event.

"Your single story is not a rough draft. It's a first edition, signed by you, worth reading cover to cover."

So here's to the ones who are on their own but not alone. To the ones who have learned that solitude and loneliness are not the same thing. To the ones who have made peace with their own company and found it surprisingly good.

You are not waiting for your life to begin. You are not incomplete. You are not too much or not enough. You are exactly where you need to be, writing exactly the story that needs to be written.

And that story? It's beautiful, even if no one else is reading it yet.

I Want to Go to Asia So Bad, It Hurts

Travel Essay • March 12, 2024

There are moments when it comes like a wave: this gentle, relentless ache to go away. Not just anywhere, but Asia. The places I hear about all the time. The places I dream about without even realizing it.

It's not escape. Not really. It's something else. Something deeper. A pull. A desire.

It's not about running away from something. It's about running toward something I can't quite name yet.

I yearn to eat soup on a plastic stool in a hot alley with the city thrumming in my ear. I yearn to lose myself on purpose in Tokyo alleys, camera strap around my neck, heart beating faster than usual.

The Dreams That Visit at Night

I dream of Bangkok markets where the air is thick with spices and stories. Where vendors call out in languages that sound like music to my untrained ear. Where every corner promises something new, something I've never seen before.

I dream of Vietnamese coffee dripping slowly while I sit and watch the world wake up. Of train rides through landscapes so different from home that they might as well be from another planet. Of temples where the air feels different, charged with something ancient and peaceful.

Seoul at midnight. The neon signs reflecting in puddles, the city that never seems to sleep, the feeling of being small in the best possible way. The street food that steams in the cold air, warming your hands and your heart simultaneously.

More Than Just Tourism

It's not about checking places off a list. It's not about the perfect Instagram shot or collecting passport stamps like trophies. It's about something more fundamental than that.

It's about wanting to understand how life can be lived differently. How people find joy in ways I've never considered. How the same human experiences - love, loss, hope, fear - play out against completely different backdrops.

"I want to be the kind of person who has been changed by places, not just visited them."

I want to learn chopstick etiquette from a grandmother who doesn't speak my language but teaches me anyway. I want to get lost in translation and find myself in the gaps between words.

The Ache of Waiting

Some days, the wanting feels almost physical. Like homesickness, but for places I've never been. For people I haven't met yet. For versions of myself that can only exist in those spaces, breathing that air, walking those streets.

I scroll through photos and videos, living vicariously through strangers' adventures, and feel this strange mix of inspiration and emptiness. They're doing it. They're there, in the places I dream about, living the experiences I crave.

But I know that when I finally go - when, not if - it won't be quite like the dreams. It will be better in some ways, harder in others. More real, more complicated, more beautiful than any fantasy could capture.

"The Asia in my dreams is perfect. The Asia I'll visit will be real. And real is always more interesting than perfect."

Until then, I collect stories. I learn phrases in languages I can't speak yet. I practice using chopsticks and research night markets and train routes. I prepare my heart for the expansion that I know is coming.

Because someday - maybe sooner than I think - I'll board that plane. I'll watch the familiar landscape shrink below me and feel my world grow larger with each mile. I'll arrive tired and overwhelmed and exhilarated, ready to transform this ache into experience.

And when I do, I know that the person who comes back won't be quite the same as the one who left. That's what I'm really yearning for, I think. Not just to see Asia, but to let Asia see me. To let it change me in ways I can't predict or control.

The ache will be worth it. All of this waiting will be worth it.

I just need to keep believing that.

Mutual Heights: Cape Town's Stone Monolith

Architecture Review • February 2024

At the very heart of Cape Town's city centre, you'll discover a stone monolith. It rises with an unmanufactured self-confidence, devoid of glass curtain walls or gleaming polished chrome. Only strength, detail, and a commanding presence define Mutual Heights.

Constructed in 1940 as Old Mutual's headquarters, this building was never meant to be subdued. Once the continent's tallest, it wore that title like a badge of pride. Every detail is measured: the carved panels, the shining brass, the marble foyer that still retains its reputation decades later.

Architecture as Statement

Walk past it and you can feel it. That old world opulence. The kind of detail missing in new buildings today. Art Deco, yes. But with a local heartbeat. Look carefully and you will find carved panels that tell stories. Depictions of South African life. Industry. Culture.

The building doesn't apologize for taking up space. It doesn't try to blend in or defer to its neighbors. It stands there, solid and unyielding, a testament to an era when buildings were meant to last centuries, not decades.

"In a city increasingly dominated by glass and steel, Mutual Heights remains defiantly itself - a reminder of what permanence looks like."

The facade tells a story in stone. Each carving, each relief, each carefully considered detail speaks to craftsmanship that has become almost extinct. This was architecture as art, building as sculpture, construction as cultural statement.

The Details That Matter

Step inside and the marble foyer envelops you. The floor patterns, geometric and precise, lead your eye upward to coffered ceilings that seem to float despite their obvious weight. The brass fixtures, now polished to a warm glow, catch the light streaming through tall windows.

The elevators, original and meticulously maintained, are works of art in themselves. The buttons, the indicators, the gentle mechanical hum - everything speaks to an era when even utilitarian elements were crafted with beauty in mind.

Every surface tells a story. Every detail serves both function and beauty. This is architecture before the false choice between practical and beautiful.

Context and Legacy

Mutual Heights exists in conversation with Cape Town itself. The building's height was once a bold statement against the sprawling, low-rise character of the city. Today, surrounded by newer, taller structures, it holds its own through sheer presence rather than scale.

The Art Deco movement found unique expression here, filtered through South African sensibilities and materials. The result is something distinctly local yet unmistakably of its international moment - a building that could only exist in Cape Town, yet speaks a global architectural language.

From street level, you understand immediately that this building takes itself seriously. It doesn't court approval or beg attention. It simply exists, confident in its own worth, secure in its place in the urban fabric.

Preservation and Purpose

The fact that Mutual Heights has survived and thrived speaks to something beyond mere historic value. The building works. It serves contemporary needs while honoring its original purpose. The spaces adapt without compromising their character.

Modern tenants occupy offices that retain their vintage character. The building has found ways to incorporate contemporary technology without sacrificing its architectural integrity. Wi-Fi and air conditioning coexist peacefully with original terrazzo floors and brass fixtures.

"The best historic buildings don't become museums. They remain vital, working parts of the living city."

Walking through Mutual Heights today, you sense the layers of time. The original vision, executed with such precision and care. The decades of use that have worn smooth the marble steps but haven't diminished the building's dignity. The careful maintenance that honors original intent while meeting contemporary needs.

This is what we lose when we tear down old buildings to make way for the new. Not just structures, but statements. Not just square footage, but stories carved in stone and cast in bronze.

Mutual Heights stands as a reminder that buildings can be both functional and beautiful, both practical and inspiring. That architecture can serve commerce while elevating the human spirit. That the built environment shapes us even as we shape it.

In a city of stunning natural beauty, Mutual Heights proves that human-made beauty has its own power, its own right to exist, its own story to tell. It reminds us that Cape Town is not just mountains and ocean, but also marble and brass, stone and steel, dreams made manifest in the urban landscape.

Why I Write: Some Feelings Just Can't Be Expressed in Conversation

Why I Write: Some Feelings Just Can't Be Expressed in Conversation

Published January 2024

Some prefer to hash things out. Some cry. Some sprint until their legs ache. Me? I write.

I do it when the silence is too loud. When words in my head start to stack up like books on the nightstand. When something small happens and, for no apparent reason, it won't leave me alone.

Writing gives shape to feelings that conversations can't always hold. It slows everything down, allowing me to catch up with myself. Some days it's just one sentence. Some days it's everything I couldn't say out loud.

The Things We Can't Say

There are thoughts too fragile for speaking. Ideas that would shatter in the air between two people, too delicate for the messiness of dialogue, too personal for the performance that conversation sometimes becomes.

Writing is where I meet myself without an audience. Where I can be completely honest because no one else is listening. Where I can explore the corners of my mind that I'm not ready to show anyone else.

"On paper, I am braver than I am in person. More honest than I am in conversation. More myself than I am anywhere else."

Some feelings are too big for words, but paradoxically, writing helps me find smaller words to hold them. Breaking down the overwhelming into manageable pieces. Making the abstract concrete, the chaotic organized.

The Rhythm of Thought

There's a rhythm to writing that matches the rhythm of thinking better than speech ever could. The pauses between sentences mirror the pauses between thoughts. The revision process mirrors how understanding deepens over time.

In conversation, once words are spoken, they're gone. They hang in the air for a moment and then disappear, taking their meaning with them. But written words stay. They wait patiently while you figure out what you really meant to say.

Writing is thinking made visible. It's the mind working through problems in real time, leaving a trail for others to follow.

I write to discover what I think about things I didn't know I had opinions about. The act of putting words on paper reveals thoughts I didn't know I was having, connections I didn't know I was making.

The Solitude of Creation

Writing is inherently solitary, and I've learned to love that solitude. It's not lonely - it's focused. It's just me and the blank page, figuring things out together.

There's no pressure to be interesting or funny or wise. No need to fill awkward silences or worry about how my face looks while I'm thinking. Just the pure act of translating internal experience into external form.

Sometimes I write things I would never say. Sometimes I write things I wish I could say but don't know how. Sometimes I write things I don't even fully understand, hoping that the act of writing will help me understand them.

The Democracy of the Page

On paper, all thoughts are equal. The profound and the mundane sit side by side, waiting to be sorted out later. Writing doesn't judge. It doesn't interrupt. It doesn't offer solutions before I've finished explaining the problem.

The page accepts everything: the false starts, the contradictions, the moments when I change my mind mid-sentence. It holds space for uncertainty, for complexity, for the messy process of figuring things out.

"Writing is how I make sense of the world, one word at a time. It's how I find order in chaos, meaning in confusion."

I write because some conversations can only happen with yourself. Because some truths can only be discovered in solitude. Because some feelings can only be fully felt when you take the time to trace their outlines with words.

The Gift of Expression

Not everyone writes, but everyone has thoughts and feelings that are difficult to express. Writing has taught me that those difficult-to-express things are often the most important things. They're worth the effort it takes to find the right words.

When I write, I'm not trying to be a writer. I'm just trying to be human in a more complete way. To honor the full range of my experience by giving it form and substance.

Writing is my way of paying attention to my own life. Of treating my thoughts and feelings as worthy of consideration, worthy of care, worthy of the time it takes to get them right.

It's how I love myself, one sentence at a time.

Why I Don't Date on Apps

Relationships • 4 min read

Let's start here: I'm not better than you. I'm not smarter, or holding onto some outdated, pre-swipe ideal of love for moral high ground. I just never downloaded one.

And that's not some holier-than-thou declaration. It's just how it happened. Or didn't happen, as the case may be.

"I like the mess of real life. I like bumping into someone when I least expect it, even if it doesn't go anywhere."

Apps fulfill their purpose. You tap the screen. You look at a face. You select based on an image and maybe six words honed over three lattes. But to me, dating websites have always been like trying to read someone through the keyhole of their profile.

The Algorithm of Attraction

There's something about the algorithmic approach to love that feels wrong to me. Not morally wrong, just... incomplete. Like trying to solve a poem with mathematics. The variables are too complex, too nuanced, too human for clean categorization.

How do you quantify chemistry? How do you swipe your way to the specific brand of weirdness that complements your own? How do you program an algorithm to recognize the kind of laugh that makes your heart skip?

Love has always been analog in a digital world. It's messy and unpredictable and doesn't follow user interface best practices.

I've watched friends spend hours crafting the perfect profile, selecting photos that tell just the right story, writing bios that strike the perfect balance between witty and approachable. It's performance art, and good performance art at that. But it's still performance.

The Serendipity of Real Life

I'm drawn to the randomness of meeting someone in line for coffee, or getting lost in conversation with a stranger at a bookstore. There's something beautiful about the unplanned nature of it all.

The universe, or chaos, or whatever you want to call it, putting two people in the same place at the same time, both of them open to possibility without specifically looking for it. That feels more romantic to me than matching based on mutual interest in hiking and The Office.

Maybe it's inefficient. Maybe it's limiting my options. Maybe I'm missing out on connections that would have been perfect if only I'd been willing to swipe right. But efficiency has never been the point of love, has it?

The Full Spectrum of Human

When you meet someone in person first, you get the whole package immediately. The way they move through space. How they treat the barista. The laugh that's too loud or too quiet. The nervous habit they don't know they have.

You can't curate real-life interaction the way you can curate a profile. There's no filter for awkward pauses or editing option for unfortunate phrasing. You get the unvarnished version of a person, and they get the unvarnished version of you.

"I want to fall for someone's flaws before I even know they're flaws. I want the messy, unfiltered, real-time version of connection."

That vulnerability feels essential to me. The risk of showing up as yourself, without preparation or optimization, and seeing if someone else responds to that authentic self.

The Pressure of Choice

There's something overwhelming about infinite options. Dating apps present you with an endless scroll of possibilities, which can make it hard to be present with any single possibility. There's always someone else to swipe to, always another profile to consider.

In real life, when you meet someone interesting, you don't have access to a catalog of alternatives. You're forced to be present with that person, that conversation, that moment. You can't comparison shop for better cheekbones or wittier banter.

Sometimes constraints create better art than unlimited freedom. Sometimes fewer choices lead to better choices.

The Timing of Things

I believe in timing in a way that dating apps seem to work against. The right person at the wrong time is still the wrong person, and no algorithm can account for the complex web of circumstances that make someone available for love.

Meeting someone organically means you're both in the same place, literally and figuratively, at the same time. You're both available to be surprised by connection. That synchronicity feels meaningful to me.

It's not that I'm opposed to intentionality in dating. I'm just more interested in intentional presence than intentional searching. Being open to connection wherever it might arise rather than hunting for it in designated digital spaces.

My Romantic Luddism

Maybe I'm a romantic luddite. Maybe I'm limiting myself unnecessarily. Maybe there's someone perfect for me out there right now, looking at their phone, wondering why no one interesting ever messages them.

But I'd rather err on the side of old-fashioned serendipity than modern efficiency. I'd rather miss out on algorithmic compatibility than lose the magic of unexpected encounter.

"Love is not a problem to be solved or a market to be optimized. It's a mystery to be lived."

So I keep showing up to life with my eyes open and my phone in my pocket. I keep believing that the right person will cross my path when we're both ready to see each other clearly.

And if that never happens? Well, at least I'll have spent my time looking at the world instead of looking at a screen, searching for love in all the analog places.

Things I Find Therapeutic That Are Free

Things I Find Therapeutic That Are Free

Self-Care • January 2024

There are days when everything has a price: time, energy, sanity. Even sleep feels like a commodity you have to earn. Those are the days I seek out the quiet, simple things that ask nothing of me but presence.

They are small, easy to overlook. But they save me more often than I'll ever admit.

Walking Without Destination

Walking without a destination. Not to exercise. Not to get somewhere. Just to walk. Just to exist in motion. I walk until the noise in my head subsides, until my footfalls find a rhythm that matches something deeper than thought.

There's something about the repetitive motion that unlocks whatever's been wound tight inside me. Left foot, right foot, breathe. The world moves past at exactly the right speed for processing. Not too fast like driving, not too static like sitting.

I notice things I never see when I'm walking with purpose: the way light falls between buildings, the sound of wind through different types of trees, the faces of strangers who are also just existing in the world.

Water in All Its Forms

Standing in the shower longer than necessary, letting hot water run over my shoulders until the tension releases. Not thinking, just feeling the heat penetrate muscle and bone.

Watching rain from inside - the percussion on the roof, the way it transforms the familiar landscape into something dreamy and new. Rain makes the world feel smaller and safer, like being inside a sound cocoon.

"Water has no agenda. It just flows, pools, falls, evaporates. It reminds me that I can be just as adaptive, just as accepting of change."

Ocean waves, if I'm lucky enough to be near them. The sound that drowns out everything else, the rhythm that's been the same for millennia. Waves remind me of the bigger picture without making me feel insignificant.

The Ritual of Tea

Making tea with full attention. Not just dunking a bag in hot water, but the whole ritual. Selecting the tea, heating the water to the right temperature, timing the steep, holding the warm mug in both hands.

The first sip that's too hot, the way the warmth travels from throat to chest to stomach. The permission to sit and do nothing but drink tea. The excuse to pause that society accepts without question.

Tea time creates a pocket of calm in the day. It's meditation disguised as a beverage break. It's self-care that doesn't announce itself as self-care.

Organizing Small Things

Arranging books by color or size. Organizing the spice cabinet. Folding laundry with unprecedented care. There's something deeply satisfying about imposing order on small, manageable spaces when the rest of life feels chaotic.

The control is real, even if it's limited. I can't organize my feelings or my future, but I can organize this drawer. And sometimes that's enough to restore a sense of agency.

External order creates internal calm. Even if it's temporary, even if it's small, it matters.

Listening to the Same Song on Repeat

Finding one song that matches the frequency of whatever I'm feeling and playing it until I've extracted every bit of emotion from it. Until I know every breath, every pause, every subtle inflection.

It's musical meditation. The repetition creates space for feelings to move through me rather than getting stuck. By the tenth or fifteenth listen, the song has done its work. The feeling has been honored, processed, released.

Writing by Hand

The physical act of forming letters, the slight resistance of pen on paper, the way handwriting forces thoughts to slow down to the speed of the hand. No delete key, no backspace. Just forward motion, one word after another.

Handwriting connects me to something ancient and human. It's the same motion humans have been making for thousands of years to record thoughts, to preserve experiences, to make sense of being alive.

The pages fill slowly, imperfectly. Crossed-out words and cramped margins tell their own story. It's messier than typing, less efficient, and somehow more real.

Watching Light Change

Sitting in one spot and watching how light moves through a space over time. Morning light is different from afternoon light, which is different from evening light. The same room becomes multiple rooms as shadows shift and colors warm or cool.

Light change reminds me that nothing is static, that even stillness contains movement. That beauty is always available if you know how to look for it. That time passing doesn't always mean time lost.

"Light teaches patience. It changes slowly enough to notice if you're paying attention, quickly enough to surprise you if you're not."

The Therapy of Presence

These things work not because they solve anything, but because they create space. Space between me and whatever's troubling me. Space to breathe, to be, to exist without explanation or justification.

They're free not just because they don't cost money, but because they free me from the pressure to be productive, to improve, to optimize. They ask nothing except that I show up, present and open to whatever arises.

"Sometimes the most therapeutic thing you can do is nothing at all. Nothing but exist, breathe, and trust that this too shall pass."

In a world that profits from our anxiety, that sells solutions to problems it helped create, these small acts of presence are quietly revolutionary. They remind me that peace doesn't have to be purchased, that healing doesn't require a subscription, that sometimes the best therapy is the simplest.

Free in every sense of the word.

17 Ways I Screwed Up My First Year as a Freelance Copywriter

Looking Expensive Without Breaking the Bank

Style • December 2024

Being chic does not mean blowing your paycheck on designer labels. It's not about spending more money, but about spending it more thoughtfully. It's about understanding what creates the impression of quality and investing in those elements strategically.

The Psychology of Expensive-Looking

What makes something look expensive? It's rarely the price tag itself, but rather a combination of factors: fit, fabric, color coordination, and confidence. The good news is that most of these factors are accessible regardless of budget.

Expensive-looking doesn't mean flashy or logo-heavy. In fact, the most expensive-looking people often wear the simplest pieces. They understand that luxury whispers; it doesn't shout.

"True elegance is about looking effortlessly put-together, like you didn't try too hard but somehow got everything right."

The Foundation: Fit is Everything

A $30 shirt that fits perfectly will always look more expensive than a $300 shirt that doesn't. This is the most important rule and the one most people ignore.

Get things tailored. Yes, even budget pieces. A simple hem or taking in a waist can transform a mediocre garment into something that looks custom-made. Many dry cleaners offer basic alterations for reasonable prices.

Know your measurements. Not your size in one particular brand, but your actual body measurements. Sizes vary wildly between brands, but measurements don't lie.

The difference between looking cheap and looking chic often comes down to the difference between the right size and the size you think you wear.

The Power of Neutral Colors

Expensive-looking wardrobes tend to be built around neutral colors: black, white, grey, beige, navy, camel. These colors are inherently sophisticated and mix effortlessly with each other.

Choose a palette and stick to it. When everything in your wardrobe works together, you always look coordinated. Coordination reads as intentionality, which reads as expense.

Invest in quality basics in neutral colors. A well-made white button-down, perfectly fitted black pants, a classic trench coat. These pieces form the backbone of an expensive-looking wardrobe.

Fabric Matters More Than Brand

Learn to recognize quality fabrics, regardless of where you're shopping. Natural fibers like wool, cotton, silk, and linen generally look and feel more expensive than synthetic alternatives.

Check the weight of the fabric. Heavier fabrics tend to drape better and look more substantial. A heavy cotton t-shirt will always look better than a thin, flimsy one.

Look for details. French seams, quality buttons, finished hems - these small touches distinguish expensive-looking pieces from obvious budget finds.

"Quality is remembered long after price is forgotten. Invest in pieces that will look good after multiple wears and washes."

The Art of Strategic Investment

You don't need to buy everything at once. Build your wardrobe strategically, investing in one quality piece at a time.

Shoes first. People notice shoes, and quality shoes last longer. Invest in classic styles in leather - they'll serve you for years and instantly elevate any outfit.

Outerwear second. A quality coat or jacket is worn over everything else and makes a strong first impression. It's worth spending more on something that will be seen frequently.

Accessories third. A quality handbag, watch, or belt can make budget clothing look expensive. Choose classic styles that won't date quickly.

Buy the best quality you can afford in pieces that matter most. Compromise on pieces that are less visible or less frequently worn.

The Power of Maintenance

Expensive-looking people take care of their things. A well-maintained budget piece can look more expensive than a neglected designer item.

Keep things clean and pressed. Wrinkled, stained, or pilled clothing immediately reads as cheap, regardless of its original cost.

Store things properly. Use hangers for structured pieces, fold knitwear, store shoes with shoe trees. Proper storage maintains shape and extends life.

Address problems quickly. Loose buttons, small tears, scuffs on shoes - fix these issues before they become obvious signs of wear.

Confidence: The Ultimate Accessory

The most expensive-looking people aren't necessarily wearing the most expensive clothes. They're wearing clothes that fit well, that they feel comfortable in, and that they wear with confidence.

Posture matters. Stand up straight, shoulders back, head up. Good posture makes everything look better and more expensive.

Own your choices. If you feel good in what you're wearing, others will assume you look good too. Confidence is magnetic and makes people less likely to scrutinize details.

"The best-dressed people aren't thinking about their clothes at all. They've found a formula that works and they execute it with confidence."

The Minimalist Approach

Less is often more when it comes to looking expensive. Expensive-looking people tend to avoid:

Logo overload. Obvious branding can actually make you look like you're trying too hard. The most expensive items often have minimal or no visible branding.

Too many trends at once. Incorporate trends sparingly. A wardrobe built on classics with occasional trendy accents looks more sophisticated than head-to-toe trend pieces.

Over-accessorizing. Choose one or two statement pieces rather than wearing everything at once. Let each piece have room to make an impact.

Shopping Strategies

Shop your own closet first. Often we have pieces we've forgotten about or haven't styled in new ways. Try new combinations before buying new items.

Quality secondhand. Vintage and consignment shops often have high-quality pieces at fraction of retail prices. Learn to spot quality construction and classic styles.

End-of-season sales. Buy winter coats in spring, summer dresses in fall. You'll get better pieces for less money if you can plan ahead.

Cost per wear. Calculate how much you'll pay per wear for expensive pieces. A $200 coat worn 100 times costs $2 per wear. A $50 coat worn 10 times costs $5 per wear.

The Long Game

Looking expensive is a long-term strategy, not a quick fix. It's about building a wardrobe of quality pieces over time, learning what works for your body and lifestyle, and developing a personal style that feels authentic.

It's about understanding that true style isn't about having the most expensive things - it's about making thoughtful choices that create a cohesive, polished appearance.

"Style is knowing who you are, what you want to say, and not giving a single care about anything else."

The most expensive-looking people often spend less on clothes than those who look obviously budget. They've learned the rules of the game and play it strategically.

They know that looking expensive is less about the price you pay and more about the thought you put into every choice. From fit to fabric to color to maintenance to confidence - it's all part of creating an impression of quality that has very little to do with what you actually spent.

That's the real secret of looking expensive: it's not about money at all.

Sustainability has left the echo chamber. And that is exactly what needed to happen.

How to Make an Employee Resign: A Manager's Guide

Let's say you have someone on your payroll who just… lingers. Not bad enough to terminate. Not worthy enough to promote. Thinks "taking the initiative" is sorting his mail before lunch. You know the guy.

You can try to lead. Coach. Give feedback. Offer guidance.

But let's be realistic: you're past that.

"You want out. You'd like them to want out. And luckily for you, there's an ancient management trick: make their life ever so slightly worse every week until they draft their own goodbye email."

It's a gentle, soft opening to the door.

This is how the experts absolutely do not recommend you do it.

1. Change Their Title Until It Disappears

Start tiny. 'Project Manager' becomes 'Support Associate.' Then just 'Associate.' Then 'Support.' Soon enough, their signature line is a passive-aggressive request for help. Eventually, they don't even put their name on emails.

2. Have Meetings That Could've Been Smoke Signals

Mandatory. Daily. Thirty minutes minimum. Don't set an agenda. Don't invite relevant people. Just talk in circles until even the walls give up. Then schedule a follow-up to review tasks undone due to the first meeting.

3. Give Feedback Using Only Vague Metaphors

"This isn't bad, but we're looking for more …heat." "I'd love to see something with sharper elbows." "Think. Less beige." Bonus points if you scrunch your face up and say, 'Just make it sing.'

4. Move Their Desk. Over. And over. And over.

Move them one desk to the left. Then to a new floor. Then to the side of the printer. Then into the break room. They'll be asking for a corner cubicle.

They'll be having meetings on a folding chair in the parking lot, screaming over the leaf blower before you know it.

5. Make Praise a Riddle

When they do it well, tell them: 'That's the kind of work we used to reward.' Or: 'Just what I wanted you to do, only… sooner.' Or the old standby: 'You've improved so much I can hardly recognize your work.'

6. Have a Performance System That Only You Understand

Create a scoring matrix. Call it "The Wheel." Measure intangible things like "grit" or "vibe." Give no guidelines. Tell them their grit score is currently 4.2, but what you're really thinking is 6.1. Step away from the situation.

7. Make "Optional" Work Look Mandatory and "Mandatory" Work Look Optional

"Ah, you didn't stay late for the optional training? Uh-huh."

"Wow, you prioritized your quarterly goals over re-doing the org chart in Comic Sans? Telling."

8. Honor Everyone Else

Order cake. Give a speech. Applaud. Not for them, of course. For Dolly, who rearranged the supply closet. Or Ian, who made it through three meetings in a day. Anything to keep attention pinched just a fraction to the left.

By now, you won't need to forward them the exit survey. They'll write their own. It'll be titled "LinkedIn."

The Real Issue

Do you actually do any of this? Of course not. But you know how a quietly struggling co-worker is often a warning that something's fundamentally broken before they give up? That's the real issue. You should just ask them. Unconventional, I grant.

"The real fix isn't making them leave. It's figuring out why they stopped showing up long before they stopped showing up."
